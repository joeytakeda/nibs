Chapter XXIII

MIGHTY panorama of golden hills swelled
like waves on all sides and vanished into cloud
like outlines of yet higher hills that zigzagged
across the horizon and merged in the west into
that matchless chain of rugged peaks. Snow
crowned, rosy under the caress of the slowly
sinking sun, bathed in a mystic veil of gilded
splendour, the Canadian Rockies were printed
like an immense masterpiece across the west
ern sky.
Hilda rode slowly along, her gaze pinned
upon the hills. Yet of them she was thinking
but vaguely. They were a familiar and well
loved presence that had been with them al
ways. To them she had turned in all her girl
ish troubles. To them she had whispered her
secrets and her dreams.
As she rode on and on, her thoughts were
all of those strange evenings in the company
of this man—the too-short, electrical half hour

Her reins hung loose over her horse’s neck;

dream. Daisy made her own trail, idly loping
along above the canyon that skirted the Ghost
River, stopping now and then to nibble at the
sweet grass along the paths.
The woods were very still and lovely. Wide
searchlights of the remaining sunshine pierced
through the branches of the trees and flickered
in and out of the woods, playing in golden,
dancing gleams upon the green growth.
Brown and gold, deeply red, burnt yellow,
and green, the trees were freighted with glori

to the ground, moved by the soft fragrant
breeze and the branches on bush and tree
seemed lazily to shake themselves, as if suc
cumbing unwillingly to the slumberous spell

Coveys of partridge and pheasants fluttered

Very near it seemed, and yet in fact a distance

point, the girl on horse paused to cast a long,

To the east, the wide-spreading pastures

It was hard indeed to tear her gaze from

She rode slowly, leisurely, but her reins
were now in her hands. In all her young life,

She pondered over the purpose of the man

Why did he not come out into the open?
